Quick Summary

Lucas Giolito has finally been signed, and it took until the MLB season already started. As low as I am on Giolito I certainly found it surprising he went unsigned this offseason. Let's break down what exactly the Padres are getting in the veteran righty.

Why the Padres Made This Move

The Padres rotation isn't in the best shape right now. As of the writing of this article, they rank 22nd in SP ERA and 18th in SP WHIP. Michael King and Randy Vasquez have been reliable this season, but the back-end of the rotation has struggled with Walker Buehler and German Marquez both having ERAs above 5 (shocking, I know). The main reason for this move however is the lack of pitching depth in the organization right now. Griffin Canning, Joe Musgrove and Yu Darvish have all been on the shelf to start the season, and Nick Pivetta joined them on the injured list about a month ago. They've been trotting out knuckleballer Matt Waldron to replace Pivetta in the rotation, and he currently has a 9.88 ERA. The other option would be JP Sears, who had a 5.47 ERA in 24.2 IP after being acquired by the team last deadline. In short, it shouldn't be hard for Giolito to be an upgrade over those guys, and I presume he's here to take over for Waldron once he's ready.

Giolito had a 3.41 ERA in 26 starts last season, that means he's prime Giolito again right? Well not quite. Giolito is yet another example of why ERA can be very misleading. There's not exactly any red on his savant page, and that's because his 3.41 ERA came with an awful 5.06 xERA and a mediocre 4.17 FIP. He carried a below average strikeout rate and below average walk rate, not an ideal combo and a far cry from what we saw from him in his prime. Long story short, he was pretty fortunate to not allow as many runs as he did. His BABIP, HR/FB% and LOB% were all above average, not something you can expect to repeat for another season.

All that being said, there's still a lot to like with Giolito. He consistently pitched deep into games for the Red Sox last season, volume the Padres could really use right now. As mentioned earlier, a mediocre starter with volume would be a sizeable upgrade to what the Padres are putting out there right now. Beyond that, I also see some potential upside in what he showed us last season. Giolito is a 3-pitch guy: 4-seam, changeup and slider. Despite the success, he had trouble commanding even 2 of those three pitches, but the rare time he did, we saw glimpses of high-end production. If he can manage to command his arsenal better, we could see more of a 23% strikeout rate with a slightly lower walk rate, which would be pretty good. There's a lot working against him, which we'll get to, but at least the upside is there.

Now the main reason (I'm assuming) he went unsigned this long is unfortunately health related. He returned in 2025 after essentially 2 years off due to TJS, and by the end of the season he was unable to pitch due to an elbow issue. There was no structural damage, but I imagine teams are concerned about whether he can consistently hold up over a full season. It wasn't just the command issues that made him frustratingly inconsistent, he had starts where his fastball velo was a tick down or his extension wasn't what it normally is. Needless to say, the situation playing out the way it did isn't gonna inspire much confidence in me. Combine that with the fact that he is getting a late start on the season and all the issues he had to begin with, I have no idea what we're gonna get.

Pitch Mix

Fastball

Giolito's 4-seam fastball was inconsistent (sensing a theme?) but there was starts where it was absolutely cooking, where he was able to average 94 mph with plus extension and vertical break. He leaned on it a lot, throwing it 48% of the time and featuring it heavily against both LHB and RHB. On the season his fastball averaged 93.3 mph, not amazing, but that did come with overall plus extension and an elite 17.2 inches of vertical break. That led to not many whiffs but plenty of called strikes and ground balls, as he was able to often get his fastball up and in on batters. It was a pitch that got the job done for him, but the question is whether he can maintain the higher end velo and extension for more than just a few games. The results were pretty encouraging when he did.
